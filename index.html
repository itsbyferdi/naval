Naval Ravikant

Naval Ravikant is an American entrepreneur and investor. He is the co-founder, chairman and former Chief executive officer (CEO) of AngelList. He has invested early-stage in over 200 companies including Uber, FourSquare, Twitter, Notion, Clubhouse, Stack Overflow, Bolt, with over 70 total exits and more than 10 Unicorn companies worth a total of 5 billion dollars.

Naval's life work

AngelList

As an angel investor, Naval Ravikant faced challenges like the fragmented and time-consuming nature of discovering startups, conducting due diligence, and managing investments. Moreover, access to top-tier deals was often restricted to a select group of well-connected individuals. Naval saw an opportunity to democratize startup investing by making it more transparent and accessible to a broader audience.

In 2010, they launched AngelList, a platform designed to streamline the fundraising and investing process. Initially, it served as a directory for startups and investors, allowing entrepreneurs to showcase their projects and investors to discover new opportunities.

As AngelList evolved, it introduced features like Syndicates, enabling investors to pool resources and back startups collectively, thus providing smaller investors with access to deals. The platform further expanded to offer tools for recruiting talent and managing venture capital funds.

Airchat

Naval Ravikant's latest venture, Airchat, was inspired by the need for deeper, more meaningful communication during the COVID-19 pandemic. Noticing the superficial nature of existing social media platforms, Naval envisioned a space for authentic voice conversations, free from the pressures of polished content. Unlike other platforms, Airchat prioritized substance over style, allowing users to engage in spontaneous, real-time discussions.

With a team of innovators, Naval developed a user-friendly interface that made it easy to join and create rooms on various topics. Airchat emphasized privacy, offering a space free from intrusive ads and algorithms. This focus on genuine, unfiltered interactions quickly attracted a diverse community of tech enthusiasts, entrepreneurs, artists, and thinkers.

For Naval, Airchat was more than a tech startup; it was a platform for authentic communication and meaningful connections.

Naval’s Almanack

The creation of "Naval's Almanack" was an organic journey, born from Naval Ravikant's shared insights on life, technology, and entrepreneurship. Naval's ability to distill complex ideas into simple, profound statements resonated with a global audience, especially through his concise tweets and in-depth podcast appearances.

Eric Jorgenson, a dedicated follower, saw an opportunity to curate and compile Naval's scattered pieces of wisdom into a cohesive guide. With Naval's blessing, Eric painstakingly organized tweets, podcast transcripts, and essays into a modern-day almanac. Naval's Almanack explores wealth creation, happiness, personal growth, and more, reflecting Naval's unique perspectives.

Naval allowed Eric creative freedom, trusting that the essence of his philosophies would shine through. The resulting book, authentic and true to Naval's voice, quickly became a sensation. It offers timeless advice in a rapidly changing world, leaving a lasting impact on anyone seeking practical guidance and philosophical insights.

The journey of Naval Ravikant

So tell me, how was Naval Ravikant's early life?

As a young boy, Naval “didn’t have many friends” and lacked confidence. His only real friends were books, which helped develop his superpower — a love of reading.

At his "after-school center," Naval read everything he could find. He started with comic books and sci-fi, then moved on to history and news, eventually delving into psychology, popular science, and technology.

Since Naval explored and reading much about psychology, science, and technology. What comes after his childhood?

He immersed himself in computer science courses and frequented the computer lab. There, he landed his first tech job, providing computer support and managing databases for Dartmouth Alumni Magazine.

Graduating with degrees in computer science and economics in 1996, Naval was passionate about tech. Before diving into the tech industry, he briefly worked as a consultant in the high-tech practice area at Boston Consulting Group.

Seems like his job at that time is aligned with his degree, how was his journey in entering the tech world?

At 22, Naval left for Silicon Valley where he took a position at the @Home Network. @Home was a high-speed cable Internet service provider with subscribers worldwide.

But, after a while working for @Home, Naval told everyone — his boss, coworkers, friends — that he was at @Home temporarily, that he’s was going to start a company.

After leaving @Home, Naval co-founded his first company, where he translated scientific ideas into business plans. He later did the same with a 3D graphics team at Intrinsic Graphics, which was acquired by Google and became Google Earth.

Wow, to be acquired by Google at that time. Who would expect if Google becomes this big today, what’s next?

He didn’t stop there. In 1999, still only 25, Naval and four others co-founded Epinions, an online review network for restaurants and movies which predated Yelp and Rotten Tomatoes.

Naval’s startup became a dot-com success and merged with Dealtime, a price comparison service. Renamed Shopping.com, it went public in 2004 and finished its first day valued at $750 million.

But this was not a fairytale exit for Naval. It was a resounding success, except for one issue: Ravikant and three of his co-founders saw none of the money. $0. Nada.

That was unexpected 😱

Yeah, I know 😔. In January 2005, Ravikant and three co-founders sued Benchmark, August Capital, and Nirav Tolia, claiming they were misled about the company’s value during the merger. They were led to believe the company was worth "$23 million to $38 million," less than the $45 million raised, rendering their shares worthless.

The lawsuit damaged Naval’s reputation in Silicon Valley, with venture capitalists calling him “radioactive mud” due to fears of future lawsuits. During this tough time, Naval’s brother Kamal moved in with him.

That was sad and depressing. Did he bounce back after those low moments?

Fortunately yes! This challenging period was crucial to Naval’s later success. The legal battle revealed the power imbalance between entrepreneurs and venture capitalists.

Naval re-entered venture capital with Venture Hacks, a blog co-founded with Babak Nivi that exposed the game theory of fundraising to entrepreneurs.

Things really went uphill after starting his blog. By 2009, Naval’s blog had grown tremendously in popularity. He then started a fund called The Hit Forge.

A defining moment came when Naval, around 35, met an investor friend who offered him a deal. This encounter sparked the idea for AngelList, revealing that many investors and startups were unaware of each other. Partnering again with Babak Nivi, they created a list of 25 investors to keep updated on startup opportunities.

This eventually grew to two lists – AngelList and StartupList. In 2010, with a list of 50 Angel Investors willing to invest $80 million in start-ups, Naval launched AngelList. His biggest success to date
